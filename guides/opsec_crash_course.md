🛡️ The Encrypted Notepad OPsec Crash Course
1. Kill the Metadata (The "BTK" Rule)
Encryption hides the text, but metadata (the data about the data) tells the story.
 * The Trap: If you export a note as a PDF or Word doc to share it, you might be exporting your username, your computer’s name, and the exact timestamp of creation.
 * The Fix: Never export files directly. Copy-paste raw text into a fresh environment, or use a "metadata scrubber" before sending anything.
2. Practice "Identity Hygiene" (The "Altoid" Rule)
The biggest threat to your anonymity isn't a hacker; it’s your younger, less-careful self.
 * The Trap: Using the same pseudonym or email for your encrypted notepad that you once used for a Reddit account, a gaming forum, or a pizza delivery 5 years ago.
 * The Fix: Assume Linkability. Keep your "Encrypted Life" entirely walled off from your "Real Life." Different browser, different email, and zero overlapping usernames.
3. Trust the Tool, Not the Connection (The "Lazarus" Rule)
Even if your notepad is end-to-end encrypted, your IP address is screaming your location every time you sync.
 * The Trap: Forgetting to turn on your VPN/Tor for just five seconds. That’s all it took to catch Sabu.
 * The Fix: Use a Kill Switch. Configure your device so that if your encrypted connection drops, all internet traffic stops instantly. Don’t let a momentary glitch unmask your home address.
4. Beware the "Analog Hole" (The "Geraldo" Rule)
Your screen is a physical object in a physical world.
 * The Trap: Taking a "screenshot" to remember something later. Screenshots often have GPS tags, and they live in your unencrypted photo cloud (iCloud/Google Photos). Worse: someone seeing your screen over your shoulder or in the reflection of your glasses during a Zoom call.
 * The Fix: If it’s sensitive enough to be in an encrypted notepad, it’s too sensitive for a screenshot. Treat your screen like a "Top Secret" document—eyes only, no cameras.
5. Don't Be the "Password.xlsx" Person
Encryption is useless if the "key" is easy to find.
 * The Trap: Storing your master password in a "Notes" app on your phone or under a keyboard.
 * The Fix: Use a dedicated Password Manager for the small stuff, but for your Encrypted Notepad? Use a Diceware Passphrase (4-6 random words) that lives only in your head.
💡 The Golden Rule of OPsec:
> Encryption protects the message. OPsec protects the sender. > If you tell the world you have a secret vault, they don't need to break the lock—they just need to follow you until you open it.
